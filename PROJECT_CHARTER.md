# Project Charter

## Project Name

Designing Affordable AI Technologies for Everyday Use in Ghana

## Vision

Make AI assistance genuinely useful for everyday Ghanaian life by meeting people in familiar languages, familiar channels, and practical daily tasks.

Confidence: 90%

Evidence:
- GhanaNLP and related resources show active work on Ghanaian language technologies.
- The project has already chosen local-language usefulness, affordability, and everyday tasks as its core direction.
- Early resource scans point to practical use cases in education, public information, finance safety, and language access.

Main uncertainty:
- The strongest first user segment still needs field validation.

## Mission

Build affordable, trustworthy AI assistants that explain, translate, summarize, and guide people through everyday information in Ghanaian English and supported Ghanaian languages.

Confidence: 90%

Evidence:
- Explaining and translating information is the common layer beneath study support, public-service navigation, financial literacy, and health navigation.
- This scope avoids premature high-risk professional advice while still giving users practical value.

Main uncertainty:
- Local-language quality must be verified by native speakers before broad testing.

## First Product Wedge

Curriculum-grounded Local-Language Study Explainer.

First slice:
"Explain this in Twi" for JHS/upper-primary school concepts, lesson notes, textbook paragraphs, revision questions, and simple checks for understanding.

Second-language test lane:
Ewe, depending on reviewer access and resource quality. Dagbani stays in the backlog unless we secure reviewers or a Northern Ghana school partner.

Confidence: 93%

Evidence:
- Twi/Akan has stronger visible resource coverage than most Ghanaian languages.
- A second-language test lane prevents the product from becoming narrowly Twi-only.
- Explain/translate/summarize/quiz flows are testable with a lightweight web prototype.
- Narrowing to school concepts reduces product sprawl and gives evaluation clearer standards.

Main uncertainty:
- The second-language choice depends on who can review outputs reliably.

What would change this:
- If available testers are mostly Ewe or Dagbani speakers, prioritize that language as the second lane immediately.

## Goals

1. Help students understand school concepts in simple language.
2. Support students, families, and teachers with study explanations, examples, summaries, and quizzes.
3. Build a reusable Ghanaian language and retrieval layer for future assistants.
4. Test affordability, usefulness, language quality, safety, and trust before scaling.
5. Produce credible documentation suitable for partners, funders, and investors.

## Non-Goals For The First Prototype

1. Do not build a full general-purpose Ghana assistant yet.
2. Do not offer professional medical, financial, legal, or government advice.
3. Do not claim fluency across all Ghanaian languages.
4. Do not deploy WhatsApp before the web flow has measurable value.
5. Do not use datasets or models commercially without license review.

## Target Users For Sprint 1

1. Junior high or senior high student.
2. Parent or guardian helping a student.
3. Teacher preparing or explaining lessons.
4. SHS 1 student catching up on weak JHS foundations.
5. Rural or peri-urban learner with limited tutoring access.

## Success Metrics

Minimum viability signal:
- 70%+ of testers complete at least one real task successfully.
- 60%+ say they would reuse it for a real need.
- Native speakers rate local-language output acceptable or better.
- No severe safety failures.
- Cost and latency look plausible for Ghanaian users, schools, NGOs, or institutional sponsors.

## Principles

1. Local language is infrastructure, not decoration.
2. Trust matters more than novelty.
3. Build the smallest useful assistant first.
4. Use Ghanaian examples without caricature.
5. Cite or ground factual answers where possible.
6. Mark uncertainty clearly.
7. Measure cost from the beginning.
