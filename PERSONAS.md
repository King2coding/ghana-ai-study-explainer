# Personas

## Recommendation

Use five school-focused Sprint 1 personas to design and test the first prototype.

Confidence: 90%

Evidence:
- These personas align with the accepted curriculum-grounded Local-Language Study Explainer wedge.
- They map directly to explain, translate, summarize, quiz, and check-understanding modes.
- They avoid broad finance, public-service, and health workflows before the study prototype is validated.

Main uncertainty:
- The actual first tester pool may be narrower than these personas.

What would change this:
- If you can recruit one group quickly, such as teachers or students, we should narrow testing around that group for the first prototype.

## Persona 1: Ama, JHS 2-3 Student

Profile:
- Age: 13-16.
- Location: peri-urban or urban Ghana.
- Device: shared smartphone or personal low/mid-range Android.
- Languages: Ghanaian English plus Twi/Akan; may understand more local language than she writes.

Needs:
- Understand school concepts explained too quickly in class.
- Convert textbook language into simpler examples.
- Practice with quiz questions.
- Ask follow-up questions without feeling embarrassed.

Useful tasks:
- Explain photosynthesis in simple English.
- Explain a science passage in Twi.
- Generate five BECE-style quiz questions.
- Summarize class notes before exams.

Risks:
- The assistant may produce overconfident wrong study answers.
- Local-language translation may be understandable but not natural.

## Persona 2: Kojo, Parent Helping A Child Study

Profile:
- Age: 30-50.
- Works long hours.
- Wants to help a child with homework but may not remember the topic well.
- Device: WhatsApp-first smartphone use.

Needs:
- Understand homework instructions.
- Explain concepts to a child in simple language.
- Translate school messages.
- Know what the child should practice next.

Useful tasks:
- Explain a homework question.
- Rewrite a teacher's message simply.
- Translate an announcement into Twi.
- Generate practice questions for a child.

Risks:
- Parent may treat the assistant as always correct.
- Needs short answers that do not feel like lectures.

## Persona 3: Adwoa, Teacher

Profile:
- Basic school or SHS teacher.
- Time-constrained.
- May teach mixed-ability students.
- Has curriculum pressure and limited prep time.

Needs:
- Create simple explanations and examples.
- Translate key terms.
- Generate classroom questions.
- Create lesson summaries or handouts.

Useful tasks:
- Explain a topic with a Ghanaian example.
- Generate warm-up questions.
- Rewrite a paragraph at JHS reading level.
- Create a short quiz with answers.

Risks:
- Teacher may need curriculum alignment; the first prototype should not overclaim curriculum compliance.
- Output quality needs fast review/editing, not blind reuse.

## Persona 4: Yaw, SHS 1 Student Catching Up

Profile:
- Age: 15-18.
- Recently entered SHS but has weak JHS foundations in science, math, or English.
- May feel embarrassed asking basic questions in class.
- Device: personal or shared smartphone.

Needs:
- Relearn foundational concepts quickly.
- Get step-by-step explanations.
- Practice with questions before class tests.
- Understand where mistakes come from.

Useful tasks:
- Explain fractions or algebra step by step.
- Compare two science concepts.
- Generate revision questions.
- Explain why an answer is wrong.

Risks:
- The assistant may oversimplify and hide important detail.
- The learner may need encouragement without the tone feeling childish.

## Persona 5: Abena, Rural Or Peri-Urban Learner With Limited Tutoring Access

Profile:
- Age: 12-17.
- Has limited access to private tutoring.
- May share a device with family.
- May prefer audio or WhatsApp later, but Sprint 1 tests text first.

Needs:
- Low-cost study help after school.
- Simple English and local-language explanations.
- Short outputs that work on low data.
- Practice questions without needing a tutor present.

Useful tasks:
- Explain a hard topic in simple language.
- Give an example from farming, market, weather, or home life.
- Summarize notes into revision bullets.
- Ask follow-up questions to find confusion.

Risks:
- A web-only prototype may not match her preferred access channel.
- Language quality and data cost will affect trust and reuse.
